SolidWorks · Surface Modeling · Personal Project

Flower
Chessboard

Type Personal Project
Software SolidWorks (self-taught)
Deliverable 6+ unique piece variations

Overview

This project started as a personal challenge: learn SolidWorks surface modeling from scratch and use it to design something that had no right to exist in CAD — a fully botanical-themed chess set.

Each piece is a reinterpretation of its traditional chess counterpart through the language of flowers: petals, stems, leaves, and organic curvature replacing the rigid towers and crowns of a standard set.

Every piece was designed from scratch using SolidWorks surface tools — no tutorials for the actual forms, just the toolset. The constraint was that each piece still had to be immediately recognizable as its chess counterpart.

The Design Challenge

The interesting tension in this project was functional legibility versus aesthetic freedom. A chess piece needs to read instantly as a king, a pawn, a knight — players can't be guessing mid-game. But the goal was to make something that felt genuinely botanical, not just "chess piece with a flower stuck on top."

The solution was to map the traditional silhouette hierarchy (tall king, medium bishops and rooks, short pawns) onto organic forms that grew from the same botanical logic — the pawn becomes a single closed bud, the queen an open blooming rose, the king a thistle.

Surface Modeling Process

SolidWorks surface modeling is meaningfully different from solid modeling — there's no history of cuts and extrudes to fall back on. Surfaces need to be built up, trimmed, and knitted together, which requires thinking about form in a more sculptural way.

I used lofted surfaces, boundary surfaces, and filled surfaces to create the organic transitions between petal forms, then knitted them into solid bodies for each piece. The board itself uses a patterned surface to suggest a garden grid rather than a hard chess grid.
